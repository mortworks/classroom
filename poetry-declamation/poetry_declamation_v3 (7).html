Session 1 — What makes a good reading? Choosing a poem

Aim: introduce meaningful, controlled delivery via professional examples; practise diction and pacing; help pupils choose a poem.

Introduction

You are preparing for the poetry declamation competition as part of Omnia Onstage.

  • You will choose a poem from the anthology you are given.
  • You will learn your chosen poem off by heart.
  • You will perform it in the class heat in two weeks’ time.

1. Sonnet 29 + Judi Dench clip

a) Present the poem:

  • Read it aloud yourself — calm, steady.
  • Explain the core movement: despair → reflection → love → transformation.

b) Set up the clip:

  • Explain the nature of the Graham Norton Show (lively atmosphere).
  • Frame the moment: she creates a still, focused space and “owns the floor” from a seated position.
Video Judi Dench – Sonnet 29 (Graham Norton clip)

A restrained, commanding delivery in a busy talk-show setting. Watch for pace, diction, and stillness.

Open on YouTube

While watching, look for:

  • pace
  • where she slows down
  • clarity of consonants
  • stillness vs gesture
  • posture
  • the mood in the studio / how the room responds

Afterwards:

  • How did she use pace?
  • Where did she slow down?
  • How clear were her consonants?
  • Did she move? If not, why not? What did you notice about her posture?
  • How did the people around her respond? What was the mood in the studio like?

2. Contrasting delivery styles

Suggested clips (links):

  • Helen Mirren reads “The Tyger”
  • Kenneth Branagh reads Sonnet 30
  • Damian Lewis reads “Words” (W. B. Yeats)
  • Poetry By Heart – choose a poem / performances

Show one or two professional readings plus one or two Poetry By Heart finalists.

Avoid “bashing” animated performances; the goal is discrimination and choice, not snobbery.

Prompt questions:

  • What do these readers have in common?
  • Where do they differ in style?
  • Did anything in the more animated readings feel added-on, or did it genuinely support the poem?
  • Which style appeals to you?

3. Microskills: diction + pacing

Diction practice

Say these clearly (don’t swallow the ending):

  • state
  • fate
  • least
  • despising
  • self
  • like
  • then
  • hope

Pacing practice (use three lines)

Read these lines aloud in different ways and decide what feels right:

  • “Wishing me like to one more rich in hope,”
  • “Desiring this man’s art and that man’s scope,”
  • “With what I most enjoy contented least;”

4. Choosing a poem

Browse the anthology and begin choosing a poem.

Criteria:

  • you understand it (at least broadly)
  • you like the sound of it
  • you can imagine speaking it aloud

In class: no online searches for readings.

5. First readings

Read your chosen poem aloud to a partner.

  • Keep it low-stakes.
  • No critique yet: this is gentle exposure and practice.

Session 2 — Learning by heart + developing delivery

1. Quick recap

  • What did we see good readers do? (and not do?)
  • What did we work on last time? (consonants, pacing)

2. Read the Whitman poem before the clip

Read silently first.

3. Dead Poets Society clip

Video Dead Poets Society – “O me! O life!” classroom scene

Mr Keating delivers Whitman with intensity and intimacy. Pupils should read the poem first so the clip lands.

Open on YouTube

After watching, discuss:

  • What do you think of Mr Keating’s delivery of the poem?
  • Where is he when he delivers it? Is this important?
  • What gesture does he use (if any)?
  • Is it a passionate delivery? Why / why not?

4. Starting memorisation

Begin learning your poem by heart.

Use whatever method works for you (quiet repetition, covering lines, whispering, testing yourself).

5. Trios rehearsal

In groups of three:

  1. Say which poem you’ve chosen.
  2. Read it aloud (it does not have to be from memory yet).
  3. Receive constructive feedback.

---

Session 3 — Class performance heat

1. Reassurance + framing + warm-up

You are judged on:

  • clarity
  • understanding
  • choices
  • impact

Remember:

  • “Drama” can come in different forms.
  • Stillness and movement/gesture are both choices.
  • You are declaiming the poem, not acting out a role.

Warm-up: recite your poem (from memory as far as possible) to a partner.

2. Performances

Each pupil performs (up to 90 seconds).

Be a respectful, attentive audience.

3. Scoring

Four categories, 5 points each:

  • clarity of voice
  • understanding of meaning
  • quality of delivery choices
  • overall impact

You may allow a class vote, but the teacher decides.

4. Strength-based feedback

You will receive at least one sentence of constructive feedback.

Announce the finalists.
